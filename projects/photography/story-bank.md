# Hadi Photography London — Story Bank

**Purpose:** A living archive of specific moments, stories, and details from real sessions. This is the raw material for every reel, carousel, blog post, caption, and DM. Generic content comes from generic sources. Specific content comes from here.

**Rule:** Raw beats polished. Write it like you'd tell it to a friend, not like you're writing a caption.

---

## The 5 Pillars

| # | Pillar | What it is | What it does |
|---|--------|-----------|--------------|
| 1 | **My Story** | Who I am, my vision, behind the scenes, photojournalism days, how I see light, emotional moments with clients | Builds the person-to-person connection. People buy from people. |
| 2 | **The Belief** | What the industry gets wrong. My point of view on the work, the day, the photography. | POV is the brand. This is what makes me different from every other photographer. |
| 3 | **Results / Client Wins** | Client transformations, testimonials in story form, the nervousness-to-ease moment, my own transformation | Turns browsers into buyers. Proof over claim. |
| 4 | **Education** | One technique, one business lesson, one thing I wish I'd known earlier | Positions me as authority without ever claiming it. |
| 5 | **The Human** | The struggle, the wins, the things I wouldn't usually talk about. Books I'm reading, walks, observations, thoughts | Makes people feel like they know me. This is why people fall in love with a brand. |

---

## How to use this bank

1. **Add stories as they come to mind.** Don't wait for a content session. Dump it here.
2. **Tag each story with one or more pillars.** A single story can serve multiple pillars.
3. **Raw is better than polished.** Don't write the caption. Write the moment.
4. **Every shoot, add one line.** After every session, the 60 seconds it takes to note the standout moment pays back every time you need content.
5. **Mark anything off-limits.** If a client wouldn't want a story shared publicly, flag it. We can still use the lesson without the specifics.

### The phone call that changed everything — "You can handle us"
- Date: 2016
- Clients: Debra (close friend) and his wife
- Location: Iran (Hadi's home at the time)
- Pillar(s): 1, 5
- Emotional tone: vulnerable, pivotal, surprising
- Usable for: reel (origin story), blog, caption, LinkedIn, about page
- Permission: public (Hadi's own story)

**The moment:**
It was almost 11pm. Hadi was sitting on the couch, months after returning from one of his toughest photojournalism assignments in dangerous zones. Mentally affected. Questioning whether to continue in journalism. The phone rang. It was Debra, a close friend getting married the next day. Their wedding photographer had been in an accident and couldn't make it. The second shooter offered but wasn't experienced enough. They needed help.

Hadi's first reaction was a hard no. He had rejected wedding photography for years. Thought it wasn't for him. Didn't want the crowds, the directing, the hassle. Then Debra's wife got on the phone. She was stressed. Debra said: "You photograph high-profile people, politicians, artists, actresses. You can handle us. Whatever happens, we are okay. We'd rather have you there than someone we don't know."

Hadi said yes. It turned out to be a great day. They loved the results. The bride recommended him to her friends. More bookings followed. That was the turning point.

**The outcome:**
The moment Hadi decided to leave photojournalism behind. "I wanted to photograph people at the start of something. No disaster, no chaos. People being happy, laughing, smiling. The nervous energy, the in-between seconds that are easy to miss but impossible to replace. These are the moments that pass the fastest and matter the most."

**Quote / exact language:**
"You photograph high-profile people, politicians, artists. You can handle us."
"I wanted to photograph people at the start of something."
"The in-between seconds that are easy to miss but impossible to replace."

### The photojournalism transfer — "the decisive moment"
- Date: ongoing (career-spanning insight)
- Clients: n/a (professional philosophy)
- Location: n/a
- Pillar(s): 1, 4
- Emotional tone: confident, authoritative, earned
- Usable for: reel (education), carousel, blog, LinkedIn
- Permission: public

**The moment:**
From photojournalism Hadi learned: instant decision-making about where to be and what to do next. Communication skills for handling different people in different situations. The ability to stay in control when everything around you is happening at once. Moving quickly to be at the right place at the right time. Henri Cartier-Bresson called it "the decisive moment."

All of this transfers directly to wedding photography. Great communication so you can handle tough situations when things don't go as planned, because they will go wrong no matter how much time you put into planning. An experienced photographer deals with that.

**The outcome:**
This is the "why I'm different" story. Not technique. Not gear. The ability to read a room, make split-second decisions, and stay calm when things go sideways. Earned in the hardest possible environment.

### Why the pre-wedding shoot became the mechanism
- Date: ongoing (evolved over years of client feedback)
- Clients: multiple (pattern across many couples)
- Location: n/a
- Pillar(s): 2, 4
- Emotional tone: thoughtful, authoritative, discovery
- Usable for: reel (education/belief), blog, carousel
- Permission: public

**The moment:**
Hadi noticed a pattern: some couples were disappointed with their wedding photos because their expectations didn't match the reality. They wanted something different but never communicated it clearly enough before the day. He started checking forums and other photographers' work and kept seeing the same complaint.

So he started offering pre-wedding shoots to all his wedding couples. Not as a bonus. As a diagnostic. A chance to talk, see each other, work together, learn from each other, and make sure they were on the same page before the wedding day. The couple learns how Hadi works. Hadi learns how to bring their best out.

The feedback was overwhelmingly positive. Over time he refined the process. It became the mechanism, not the add-on.

**The outcome:**
This is the origin of The Presence Method. The pre-wedding shoot exists because couples were getting photos they didn't want, and the root cause was that photographer and couple never properly understood each other before the day.

### The staged shoot problem — "Weddings have friction"
- Date: ongoing (career observation)
- Clients: n/a (industry belief)
- Location: n/a
- Pillar(s): 2
- Emotional tone: frustrated, authoritative, protective
- Usable for: reel (confession/belief), carousel, LinkedIn, caption
- Permission: public

**The moment:**
As cameras get cheaper and more advanced, Hadi sees more people picking up professional cameras and calling themselves wedding photographers after a handful of styled shoots. Staged sets, models, perfect lighting, zero real-world pressure. Completely misleading about what they are actually capable of.

A staged shoot has no friction. Weddings do. Bad weather. Uncooperative guests. Timeline pressure. Moments that happen once and never again. A photographer who has only worked in controlled, staged conditions will struggle when reality hits. Most couples discover this when it's too late.

Many couples assume all photographers deliver roughly the same result and choose based on the nicest portfolio at the lowest price. That often leads to hiring someone under-experienced, under-insured, or without a proper agreement.

**The outcome:**
This is a Pillar 2 cornerstone. The belief that wedding photography is not for everyone. It takes real experience to handle the greatest day of a couple's life. That's a responsibility, not a gig.

**Quote / exact language:**
"A staged shoot has no friction. Weddings do."
"Moments that happen once and never again."
"Most people discover this when it's too late."

### The night-before advice — "Stare at each other. A lot."
- Date: ongoing (given to every couple)
- Clients: n/a (universal advice)
- Location: n/a
- Pillar(s): 4, 2
- Emotional tone: intimate, calm, surprising, warm
- Usable for: reel (education), carousel, caption, pre-shoot briefing email
- Permission: public

**The moment:**
The day before every shoot, Hadi tells his couples two things:

1. Spend the night together. Relax. Unwind. Do what you love. Stare at each other, a lot. Look each other in the eyes. Reconnect. Breathe deeply.

2. Let go of your expectations. Embrace the spontaneous, the weather, the wind, the laughter, whatever unfolds. Every shoot is unique. There is no set pattern. It is all about you.

**The outcome:**
No other photographer gives this advice. Most send a logistics email: what to wear, where to park, what time to arrive. Hadi sends an emotional preparation brief. The couple arrives having already reconnected, already present, already looking at each other differently. The session starts before the camera comes out.

**Quote / exact language:**
"Stare at each other. A lot."
"Let go of your expectations. Embrace the spontaneous."
"Every shoot is unique. There is no set pattern. It is all about you."
