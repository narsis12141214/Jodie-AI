# CONTACT FORM {#contact-form}

**Why it matters:** Your contact form is the first time this couple will be reaching out to you, and it is a critical step that sets the tone for communication during the rest of the inquiry-to-booking process. You’ll use the info that this form collects to craft your initial inquiry response, which is the first opportunity you have to make a personal connection with them. How you’re able to personalize this critical email depends entirely on the questions you ask in this form. You can’t address or reply to information that you don’t have\!

**What to include:**   
The length of your contact form will generally determine the quality and quantity of the inquiries you receive. All else being equal, the more questions you ask, the fewer inquiries you will get (because many people get overwhelmed by long forms)—and the fewer questions you ask, the more inquiries you will get. So make sure you really think through what actually matters and remove any difficult questions that would require them to do research or think too hard about how to answer. However, it’s also important to collect certain necessary information, and some questions are important to help determine if a couple reaching out to you is going to be a good fit for your services. 

Generally, here’s what your form needs to accomplish:

* Request the names and pronouns of both partners  
* Ask questions that will enable you to determine if you’re available—typically their general location and preferred time frame  
* Request their contact information—at least 1 email (although 2 is better in case someone mistypes) and at least 1 phone number (in case an email goes to spam).  
* Asking where they live can give you information about whether they are getting married locally to them and what their timezone is for a consultation call  
* Include one “value creation question” that will help you understand their vision, needs, desires, fears, stressors, and what are their motivators for having the type of wedding day they are envisioning so that you can demonstrate your expertise and the value of your services in your response.  
* Include one personal question that is not too invasive and easy to answer, but will help you personalize your response and start to build a connection with them.

**Pro-tip:** If you want a better idea of how long your contact form takes to fill out, have a friend fill it out as if they were a potential client and ask them to time themselves\! You can also ask for their feedback on what questions were the hardest to answer.

# THANK-YOU PAGE {#thank-you-page}

**Why it matters:** Inquiring clients should be redirected to a “Thank-You Page” on your website once they fill out your inquiry form. While many photographers skip this step, it’s important for your inquiry-to-booking process because the moment a couple fills out your inquiry form, they’re super excited—and if they aren’t redirected to a page after they hit “submit,” they don’t know for sure if their inquiry went through and might not know what to do next. A “Thank-You Page” should take advantage of that momentum and keep a couple feeling excited rather than leaving them feeling confused. 

**What to include:**

* Confirmation their inquiry went through  
* Explanation of next steps and expectations for when they’ll hear back from you personally  
* Something for them to do in the meantime (encourages engagement with your brand by linking to your most popular content or asking them to follow you on social media)

**Pro Tip:** If you are sending an automated email reply to your contact form (recommended) the thank-you page is a very important opportunity to tell couples to check their email immediately.

# INITIAL INQUIRY EMAIL RESPONSE {#initial-inquiry-email-response}

**Why it matters:** Your initial inquiry email is arguably the most important email in your inquiry-to-booking process and it’s critical to have a unique, on-brand base template that you can quickly personalize for each couple that contacts you and reply as fast as humanly possible. Why? Because most couples reach out to more than just one photographer, and a delayed response or a boring templated response (that sounds the same as another photographer) will make their first experience with you fall flat and feel impersonal. You want to make a memorable first impression that stands out from the crowd, so the more you can tailor this response to each inquiry and ensure that your base template is unique and on-brand—the better. We can’t stress out of all of the templates in your entire workflow, how crucial it is that this one is unique to you—which is why we’ve included multiple prompts to help you make this email your own and completely personalize it for each couple.

**What to include:**

* A completely customized first paragraph for each and every inquiry. Be personal, excited, show you care right from the beginning, and include details from what they’ve shared with you so it’s clear this is not a templated response. Your contact form will need at least 1 personalized question to do this effectively. Definitely do not start your response with “congratulations on your engagement” or “thanks so much for contacting me”—there are so many other photographers and templates that have this as their first line of their inquiry response email. 

* Information that’s valuable to the couple. The strategy here is to start treating the couple like they already hired you. This will set you apart from other photographers they may be simultaneously contacting and also trigger a psychological phenomena called “reciprocity” where they feel indebted to you for your generous help. Don’t be afraid to show off your expertise—it’s important to communicate that you are an authority they can trust. The “value” you provide in this initial response could be insider knowledge about the venue they are considering based upon your prior experience shooting there or general guidance that will help or support them.

* Personal and meaningful information about who you are. Couples will be spending several hours with you on one of the most important days of their lives, and having a personal connection and comfortability with their photographer is a really important part of most couples' decision on who they hire. Start by building that connection from your very first email.

* Full gallery examples. If you have some really stand-out galleries of weddings relevant to their inquiry, sharing them will help set you apart. If you’ve photographed a wedding at the venue they’re considering and would like to work there again, including that gallery in this email will instantly give you points over other photographers they might be considering.

* An explanation of the next steps and why a consultation call is important. The ultimate goal of the initial response to an inquiry is to get the couple to schedule a consultation call with you (whether you prefer phone, video, an in-person meeting, or letting couples choose). If you aren’t currently doing personalized consultations with your couples before booking, we can guarantee you’re leaving money on the table (not to mention a crucial opportunity to set yourself apart, connect with your couple, and tailor your marketing messaging directly to them). When it comes to the logistics of trying to get a couple to schedule a consultation with you, it’s important to make it as easy as possible for them. Many couples prefer the ease of being able to quickly use a scheduling app (like Calendly or your CRM), but also including your available times in the email can make it feel more personal.

* Pricing information (see note below)

**A note about what pricing information to share:** There is a lot of debate about when and where is best to share a pricing guide laying out all of your packages/collections with all prices listed—whether you should put it all on your website, share it in an initial inquiry response, or wait for a consultation call so you can customize your offerings to your client.

Research and testing shows that not sharing any pricing information on your website frustrates couples and leads to less inquiries. On the other hand, sharing all of the nitty gritty details of everything you offer on your website can overwhelm and confuse couples and it also means they have no reason to inquire and get further details. Even the best website in the world can't convey the true value of \*you\*, your personality, your care, and how you will connect to your clients, so we recommend sharing starting prices on your website and clearly explaining what your packages/collections include so that clients have a "ballpark" of what to expect, but there’s no need to overwhelm them with every detail yet. 

When it comes to an initial inquiry response, whether you choose to include your full pricing guide or not will depend on the volume of inquiries you get, your availability for consultation calls, and how much you want to increase the chances of booking the inquiry. 

* **If you have a lower inquiry volume or want the to have the highest chances of booking an inquiry:** we suggest taking a similar approach to what’s recommended for your website, and including your starting price and the important things of value that your packages include while incentivizing clients to get on a consultation call so you can get a better sense of their personal needs, fears, worries, stressors, goals, and desires and can walk them through your full pricing information and also offer a custom option if that suits them best.   
* **If you have very high inquiry volume or are very busy and absolutely need to pre-qualify your inquiries as much as possible:** you can include a link to your full pricing information with all of your packages listed in this email so that couples can compare rates with you and other photographers directly and make an initial decision on whether you will be a good fit for their budget or not before scheduling a consultation call with you.

A final key tip on any pricing information you share is to make sure that it’s a link and not a PDF or image attachment. **Attachments are a huge red flag for most spam filters** and will massively increase the likelihood that your response to your client will not make it to their inbox.

**When to send:** As soon as humanly possible (within 12 hours if you can). Truly, this is the one email worth sending during an evening or weekend (if at all possible—or have an assistant or VA do it for you). Why? Statistically, 30-50% of sales go to the vendor that responds first, so the sooner you reply, the better chance you have of booking (just imagine: if a couple reaches out to 3 photographers in an hour, you’ll be much more likely to get the job if you respond first). The maximum response time to a couple’s inquiry should be 1 business day. I constantly get compliments at Adventure Instead about my response time to initial inquiry emails, and it truly makes a difference in how many couples book me.

**Pro tip:** Once you’ve created your own unique, on-brand base template for your initial inquiry response, practice customizing it for your latest inquiry and then send it to yourself. Make sure you look at it on both desktop and mobile and ensure it’s formatted correctly and easy to scan and read. Make sure you don’t have any mismatched font sizes or anything that would indicate that this email came from a template. Like your contact form, we also strongly suggest having a friend review your initial inquiry response as if they were a potential client and give you feedback on its value, readability, and how much it incentivizes them to schedule a consultation with you. 

**Subject line ideas:** Wondering what your subject line should be for this initial inquiry response? As with the rest of the email, it is vital to write a unique subject line for every inquiry you receive. 

**Here’s what you should keep in mind:**

* Many photographers respond to an inquiry from their CRM or website platform—the issue with this is that the subject line reads something along the lines of “RE: Submission \- New Inquiry” or “RE: Form Submission.” When that email lands in a couple’s inbox, they might not recognize what the email is about or who it is from… and move it straight to spam. 

* Don’t use a subject line from a template that someone has given you or the same subject line as another photographer because it can dramatically decrease your open rates for the email and is more likely to be flagged as spam.

* In short, create an easily personalized subject line specific to YOUR business and YOUR couple. **Emails with personalized subject lines are 26% more likely to be opened.** We encourage you to put your business name in the subject to guarantee it is exclusive to you and to tailor it with information from the couple’s inquiry such as: “\[Date\] \[Location\] wedding with \[Your company name\]” or “\[Name \+ Name\] \[Location\] wedding \- \[Your company name\].”

* If you want to really stand out with a stellar subject line, we suggest brainstorming a list of 10-20 possible subject lines—wait a day or two, and then come back and pick out the best one(s) that would make you want to click on the email. You can also test different subject lines to see which ones actually have higher “open rates” (the percentage of people who open your emails) if that’s something your system has the ability to track.

# INITIAL INQUIRY TEXT MESSAGE {#initial-inquiry-text-message}

**Why it matters:** Why send a text if you’ve already sent an email? Here are some fun stats:

* Only 20% of emails are opened, whereas 98% of text messages are opened   
* Only 6% of emails are responded to, whereas 45% of text messages are responded to  
* It takes the average person 90 minutes to respond to an email versus 90 seconds to respond to a text message  
* 95% of all text messages are read within 90 seconds (most are read in under 5 seconds)

Sending this text message in addition to your initial inquiry response via email will make it much more likely the couple will see your message sooner, open it, and reply while they’re still excited. This keeps you from getting ghosted as often, is a great way to double-check your email response didn’t end up in spam (this can happen frequently if you send your replies through a CRM; customer relationship manager), and confirms the email address is correct (in case the couple accidentally mistyped their email address in your contact form). Plus, more and more couples are planning their wedding day on the go and many find it easier and more personalized to communicate with vendors via text message. 

**Pro tip:** If you don’t want to communicate with couples via text with your personal phone number, you can get a [Google Voice number](https://support.google.com/voice/answer/115061?hl=en&co=GENIE.Platform%3DDesktop) and use it to text (or call).

# 1ST INQUIRY FOLLOW-UP {#1st-inquiry-follow-up}

**Why it matters:** Did you know you are twice as likely to make a sale if you follow up? Why? Because 48% of people selling services never follow up with their inquiries. Imagine this: a couple, both engineers who work at the same company, inquire with you on a Sunday night. You reply 12 hours later, and your email lands in their inbox Monday morning—which is when their company announces a huge project due on Wednesday. They find themselves swamped with work, too busy to check their email, and forget to reply to your text message. By the time the project is finished on Wednesday, your initial inquiry response is buried 100+ emails deep. 

But sending a follow-up 3 days later brings that email right back to the top of their inbox, so it can be at the front of their minds when their project is done. That Wednesday night, they see your follow-up and finally schedule a consultation with you (which is the main goal of this follow-up).

If you don’t follow up, they might never get around to seeing your initial inquiry response—even if they had all the best intentions and loved your work. 

**What to include:**

* What you’re following up on   
* Clear call-to-action (CTA) that outlines next steps & scheduling link for the initial consultation (phone call, video chat, or in-person meeting)

**When to send:** At Adventure Instead, I send this follow-up 3 days (72 hours) after a client inquires if I have not gotten any kind of email or text response or seen them book a call. This follow-up is sent in the same thread as the initial inquiry email so it brings the initial inquiry information back to the top of their inbox—so I make sure it has the exact same subject line (otherwise it will start a new chain). If you feel this timing is too pushy/aggressive for the given situation, you can slow it down to 5-7 days after they initially inquired.

# 3RD & FINAL INQUIRY FOLLOW-UP  {#3rd-&-final-inquiry-follow-up}

**Why it matters:** If a couple hasn’t gotten back to you after 2-3 weeks—they probably got insanely busy, realized they actually needed more time before they felt ready to book a photographer, or feel so guilty about not replying to your other emails they are nervous to respond so much later. 

This email is intended to release them from any guilt, to inform them that they won’t be getting any future emails from you, and to let them know you are still open to working with them in the future. It officially “closes” their inquiry with you and therefore communicates a bit of scarcity (so they know you’re not waiting around forever), which creates a low-key push to respond if they’re still interested.

**What to include:**

* Speak to the process—note that you’ll be closing/canceling their inquiry  
* Best wishes for their future

**When to send:** At Adventure Instead, I send this follow-up 14 days (2 weeks) after a client inquires if I have not gotten any kind of email or text response or seen them book a call. This follow-up is sent in the same thread as the initial inquiry email so it brings the initial inquiry information back to the top of their inbox—so I make sure it has the same subject line (otherwise it will start a new chain). If you feel this timing is too pushy/aggressive for the given situation, you can slow it down to 21 days (3 weeks) after they initially inquired.
