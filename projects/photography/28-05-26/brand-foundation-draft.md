## 1. Vision Statement

**My vision is to give every couple I photograph the calmest, most present version of the most important day of their life — and to leave behind a body of work from London that proves what is possible when a photographer treats people first and pictures second.**

*Source:* Story bank entries #2 (the phone call, "I wanted to photograph people at the start of something"), #19 ("Technical skill gets you hired. Emotional skill gets you remembered."), #14 (the night-before advice). The two threads that run under everything Hadi has ever said about this work: photographing people at the start of something good after a career photographing the opposite, and the body of London work itself as the legacy.

## 2. Mission Statement

**My mission is to lead each couple through a deliberate process — a pre-wedding conversation, a pre-wedding shoot, and a wedding day run by someone who has spent twenty years learning how to read a room — so that the people in front of the lens forget the camera is there and the photographs follow as a result.**

*Source:* The pre-wedding shoot as mechanism (entry #5), "Authentic emotion is created by conditions, not commands" (entry #19), the photojournalism transfer / decisive moment (entry #4).

## 3. Core Values

Pulled from recurring language in the story bank, the 17 reviewed Google reviews, and Hadi's own framing of the work.

**1. Presence over performance.**
The photographer's job is to manage the room so the couple stops performing for the camera and starts being themselves. Hadi sends a "stare at each other, a lot" brief the night before a shoot. Most photographers send a logistics email. (Source: entry #14.)

**2. Earned calm.**
Twenty years of decision-making in unforgiving conditions — photojournalism, then weddings — produces a particular kind of calm in the room. It is not a personality trait. It is a skill. It is what couples are actually paying for. (Source: entries #4, #8.)

**3. Direction, not documentation.**
Hadi guides people through the shoot. He gives clear instruction, mid-session feedback, posing direction, simple actions that let real reactions surface. The reviews use the words "guided," "taught," "walked me through," "directed" — 5 separate clients independently. (Source: entry #9 Pattern 3.)

**4. Honesty about what the work costs.**
Hadi will tell a couple their wedding photographer should not be picked by lowest price, that a staged shoot has no friction, that some photographers cancel 48 hours out. He says the quiet thing out loud. (Source: entries #7, #10, Josh and Catherine #15.)

**5. The couple is the subject. Always.**
Not the dress, not the venue, not the light, not the photographer's portfolio aspirations. The couple. Every other choice serves them. (Source: entry #14 "It is all about you," entry #19 "Your presence changes the room.")

## 4. Specialty (sharpened)

**Hadi's specialty: a small, deliberate London elopement and pre-wedding shoot for couples who are quietly nervous about being photographed and want a photographer who will lead them through it.**

Not "London elopement photographer." Too broad — every London elopement photographer says that. The sharper specialty narrows on three vectors at once:

- **Size:** small / elopement / micro wedding (under 30 guests)
- **Location:** London (the body of work, the local knowledge, the 100 five-star reviews are all here)
- **Type of couple:** couples who arrive nervous, camera-shy, or quietly self-conscious — and want a photographer who actively guides them rather than "documents" from a distance

This is supported by the strongest single signal in the brand: 11 of 17 reviewed Google reviews use the nervousness-to-ease language unprompted. That is not a positioning choice. It is a description of what the work already does.

The Maddie Mae framework recommends combining 2–3 categories. This combines **size + location + type of couple** — the three sharpest cuts for Hadi's actual book of work.

## 7. Unique Value Proposition

**Draft UVP:**

**For couples who quietly dread being photographed on the day that matters most, I am a London elopement photographer who runs the room — pre-shoot conversation, mid-session direction, twenty years of reading people in high-pressure conditions — so the camera disappears and you actually get to be there.**

Why this passes the "sea of sameness" test:
- "Quietly dread being photographed" is specific — most UVPs say "feel comfortable" or "be yourself" without naming the fear. This names the fear.
- "Runs the room" is Hadi-specific language pulled directly from reviews ("walked me through," "guided," "directed"). No other London photographer's UVP uses that phrase.
- "Twenty years of reading people in high-pressure conditions" is a defensible single claim that very few London elopement photographers can make. Photojournalism background is rare in this market.
- "The camera disappears and you actually get to be there" maps to the nervousness-to-ease transformation, the single strongest signal in the reviews.

No banned words. No "intimate," "moments," "captured," "story," "authentic," "documentary." No "luxury," "cinematic," "high-end."

A second, shorter alternative for use in headlines:

**"London elopement photography for couples who want to be in their day, not perform it."**

## 8. Brand Voice Recommendation

**Recommended voice: Straight No Chaser, with one element borrowed from Refined Dreamer.**

The choice is not "Refined Dreamer" — although the existing copy leans that way. The problem with Refined Dreamer for Hadi is that it puts the photographer in the position of the aesthete writing poetic descriptions of feelings, and Hadi's actual voice is much more direct than that. Look at how he talks in the story bank:

- "A staged shoot has no friction. Weddings do." (entry #8)
- "You're not paying for 8 hours of photography. You're paying for 20 years of experience compressed into one day." (entry #8)
- "Most people notice the kiss, the hug, the laugh. I notice the half-second before that." (entry #18)
- "Stare at each other. A lot." (entry #14)
- "I don't just see people. I see anticipation." (entry #18)

That is Straight No Chaser. Short sentences. Direct claims. Earned authority. No hedging. No flowery construction.

The Refined Dreamer element to borrow: a willingness to slow down and describe a single specific observation when the moment calls for it — the tube observation, the half-second before the kiss, the breath in. Used sparingly, this is what stops the voice from being purely transactional.

**Three sentences in the chosen voice (Straight No Chaser):**
- "Most couples arrive nervous. The good ones admit it. The rest of the day takes care of itself."
- "I have spent twenty years learning how to be in the right place at the right second. That is the job."
- "I send my couples one thing the night before the shoot: stare at each other, a lot. That is the brief."

**Contrast — the same idea in the other three voices, to show what we are choosing against:**

- **Refined Dreamer:** "There is a quiet, almost imperceptible shift that happens in a couple before the camera comes up — the breath, the lean, the slow turn of a shoulder. That is where the photographs live."
- **Client BFF:** "Honestly? Most of my couples turn up a bit nervous and I love that, because it means we get to break the ice together before the camera even comes out."
- **Hype Human:** "These shoots are SO MUCH FUN — by the end you are going to be wondering why you were ever worried about being on camera in the first place!"

The Hype Human voice is obviously wrong for Hadi. The Refined Dreamer is what the existing site already does and it has produced underperforming CTR (0.4% sitewide vs industry 2–3%, per 27 May SEO pipeline). The Client BFF voice is too casual for the £1,400–£5,100 commission range. Straight No Chaser fits the work, the price point, and the way Hadi actually speaks.

## 9. Brand Story (StoryBrand framework)

**Character:**
A couple in their thirties planning a small London wedding or pre-wedding shoot. Educated, considered, quietly nervous about being photographed. They want the day to feel like them.

**Problem — external:**
They cannot tell which London elopement photographer to trust. The portfolios look similar. The pricing is opaque. The wedding industry pushes them toward a louder, more performative day than they want.

**Problem — internal:**
They are afraid they will look stiff in the photos. They are afraid they will spend the entire day self-conscious about being watched, and end up with images that prove it. One of them is more reluctant than the other (entry #11 pattern) and is privately dreading the shoot.

**Problem — philosophical:**
The day that should be theirs has been colonised by an industry that treats couples as decoration for the photographer's portfolio. The photographs should be of the couple. They are increasingly of the venue, the dress, the light, and the photographer's aesthetic choices.

**Guide:**
Hadi. Empathy: he has spent twenty years watching nervous people on the most important days of their lives, and he has noticed the same thing every time — they relax when someone runs the room for them. Authority: photojournalism in difficult environments, 100 five-star Google reviews, 11 of 17 reviewed clients independently using the same nervousness-to-ease language, repeat international clients who hired him sight unseen.

**Plan:**
1. **A real conversation before anything is booked.** Hadi learns what the couple is actually nervous about. (Source: the camera-shy husband, entry #11.)
2. **A pre-wedding shoot as the mechanism, not the add-on.** A low-stakes session where the couple learns how Hadi works and Hadi learns how to direct them. (Source: entry #5.)
3. **A wedding day run by someone who reads rooms for a living.** Clear direction, calm presence, the decisive moment training. (Source: entries #4, #19.)
4. **A night-before brief that is not logistics.** "Stare at each other. A lot." (Source: entry #14.)

**CTA:**
Send an enquiry. Have the conversation. See if it fits.

**Avoided failure:**
- Stiff, self-conscious photos that do not look like them
- A day spent performing for a camera instead of being in the moment
- Being treated as a client number rather than two specific people
- The disappointment of paying premium prices for generic London elopement images

**Achieved success:**
- A photographer they actually like
- A pre-wedding shoot where they noticed something new about each other (entry #1, "It felt like falling in love again")
- A wedding day where the camera stopped existing for them
- Photographs they will not flinch at in ten years
- A relationship, not a transaction (Josh and Catherine, the thank-you booking, the 10th anniversary couple)

**Tension 1 — the specialty is narrower than the current pricing structure assumes.**
The Presence Method packages include The Odyssey at £5,100 for 10-hour / destination / multi-day work. That is a full wedding, not a small London elopement. A US couple hiring sight unseen needs a different first email than a London couple who can have coffee with Hadi. The current single inquiry workflow does not address this.
