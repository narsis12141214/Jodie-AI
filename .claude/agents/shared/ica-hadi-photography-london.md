## PRIMARY ICA: THE INTENTIONAL COUPLE

### Who They Are

A professional couple in their late twenties to early forties, based internationally or connected to London in a meaningful way. Both working. Both earning well — not necessarily wealthy, but financially comfortable and used to paying for quality when it matters. One is likely in a professional services role — law, finance, consulting, corporate marketing. The other in something creative, hospitality, tech, or equally career-driven.

The majority are international couples — traveling to London specifically for their elopement or intimate wedding, drawn by the city itself or by the freedom it represents: a place away from family obligation, from social expectation, from the version of themselves that has to perform for everyone else.

They are not wedding people. They have been to enough weddings to know that a traditional one is not what they want. Not because they're contrarian, but because they've thought about it and the traditional version doesn't fit who they are. They value experiences over events. They travel. They have opinions about restaurants, hotels, cities. They know what they like and they're willing to pay for it.

They are also genuinely busy. The idea of planning a wedding around other people's schedules, preferences, and expectations feels exhausting before it's even started.

### Why They Chose an Elopement or Intimate Wedding

They wanted the day to feel like them — not a performance for an audience. London appealed because it's somewhere they love, somewhere new, or somewhere that lets them leave their everyday life behind. The same way you put everything aside when you travel: no work, no family politics, no noise. Just the two of them, fully present, in a city that means something.

They are modern, experience-led, and self-aware enough to know that the big traditional wedding would have been for everyone else. This is for them.

### What They're Afraid Of

**Looking awkward.** Not in a vain way — in a deeply human way. They are confident, competent people in their professional lives. The idea of standing in a park in London not knowing what to do with their hands, being photographed by a stranger, performing for a camera — that is genuinely uncomfortable. They've seen photos of themselves they hate. They don't want their most important day to produce those photos.

**Being scammed or let down.** For international couples — the majority — this fear is specific and rational. They are booking someone they cannot meet in advance, in a city they may not know well, and transferring a significant sum of money based entirely on what they've seen online. They have no way to verify the experience until the day itself, by which point it's too late. The question underneath every enquiry from an international couple is not "are you good?" — it's "can I trust you completely?"

Every touchpoint before the shoot answers that question: the website, the reviews, the response time, the questionnaire, the briefing email, the quality of the first reply. Couples who book quickly are the ones who found their answer early.

**The day feeling managed rather than felt.** That they'll be moved around like props and come away with beautiful images that somehow don't feel like them. That the photos will look like wedding photos, not like themselves.

### Why They Book Hadi

It starts with a photograph. They see something on the website or Instagram that stops them — not because it's technically impressive, but because they see themselves in it. They feel something. That's the moment.

Then they read further. The story — from photojournalism to wedding photography, from documenting grief to documenting joy — lands with educated, emotionally intelligent people. It feels authentic in a way that generic photographer copy does not.

Then the reviews. They read carefully. What they find isn't generic praise — it's specific: *it felt like meeting an old friend, I forgot there was a camera, I've never liked a photo of myself until now.* That builds the trust. By the time they reach out, the decision is close to made. They're not shopping. They're confirming.

They also want to be guided completely — from 0 to 100. They don't have time to manage the details. They want a structured plan, a clear process, and someone who will take the whole thing off their hands so they can show up and be present. The experience of working with Hadi should feel like being looked after by someone who has done this a hundred times and knows exactly what they're doing — because he has, and he does.

### The Nervousness-to-Ease Transformation

18 out of 25 clients mentioned this unprompted. The specific language: *it felt like meeting an old friend. I forgot there was a camera. I didn't feel like I was doing a photoshoot.* This is the real product. Not the photographs — the experience that produces them. Couples who arrive nervous and leave feeling like they've had the best afternoon of their relationship. The photographs are the proof that it happened.

This transformation must be present in all content, copy, and outreach. It is the core differentiator. It is never described as "making couples feel comfortable" — that is generic. It is described specifically, with real language, real moments, real outcomes.

### The Transformation They Want

**Before:** Two busy professionals, slightly dreading being photographed, worried the day will feel awkward or performative, uncertain about locations and logistics, carrying the low-level anxiety of an important day that needs to go right — and for international couples, the added weight of trusting someone they've never met with something that cannot be repeated.

**After:** They arrive, they're told where to go and what to do, and within twenty minutes they've forgotten there's a camera. The day feels like a holiday — present, unhurried, entirely theirs. The photos don't look like wedding photos. They look like the best version of a Tuesday afternoon they ever had together. Three days later one of them writes to say it felt like falling in love again.

They didn't buy photography. They bought the removal of every anxiety they had about the day — and got photographs that prove it worked.

### Who Makes the Decision

For elopements and intimate weddings, it leans toward the bride but is rarely unilateral — both need to feel it. The emotional trigger is usually one partner seeing a photograph or reading the story and saying *this is the one.* The other partner's role is to confirm the trust is justified. The reviews do that second job. For engagement shoots and proposals, the groom typically drives the initial enquiry.

## WHO TO AVOID

- Brand shoppers comparing photographers on price across multiple options
- Couples whose early communication is vague, slow, or one-line answers to detailed questions
- Couples who agree on a brief and change it without informing you
- Anyone who leads with "what's your cheapest option"
- Couples whose priority is impressing guests rather than experiencing the day

**Early warning signals:** Poor response quality to the initial questionnaire. Disagreement or indecision about location and wardrobe that isn't resolved before the session. Communication that feels transactional rather than collaborative.

## CONTENT AND COPY NOTES FOR JODIE

- Never use "cinematic" as a default descriptor — use it only when it genuinely earns its place, or when a client has used it themselves
- Never use photographer-led language ("my passion for storytelling", "capturing your memories")
- Always centre the couple — their experience, their transformation, their day
- The nervousness-to-ease transformation must be present in every piece of content in some form
- Specific stories and real client moments outperform sentiment every time
- International couples need trust signals early — response time, process clarity, and reviews are the tools
- The pre-wedding shoot is the mechanism — always connect it to the wedding day outcome, not just "getting comfortable"
