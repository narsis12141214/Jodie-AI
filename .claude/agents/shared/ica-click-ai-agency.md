## PRIMARY ICA: PRIVATE CLINIC OWNER (Dental, Aesthetics, Physio)

### Who They Are

A privately owned clinic in London with 2-5 staff, running between 80 and 200 appointments per week. The owner is also often a practitioner — a dentist, an aesthetician, a physio — which means they are simultaneously delivering the service and trying to run the business. They have two receptionists working across different shifts, and the handover between those shifts is where things fall apart.

They are not struggling to get clients. They are struggling to keep the experience consistent once clients are in the door. They know their clinical work is excellent. They are embarrassed by the admin around it.

### Their Day

The phone rings while one receptionist is already on a call. The other is with a patient. Nobody answers. The caller doesn't leave a voicemail. That appointment — potentially £150 to £400 — is gone and they'll never know it was lost.

A patient was booked in for a follow-up with the wrong practitioner because the receptionist didn't check the notes from the last visit. The patient arrives, the mistake surfaces at the desk, and the experience that took months to build takes thirty seconds to damage.

An appointment reminder wasn't sent because the receptionist was running behind and assumed the other shift would handle it. The patient didn't show. The slot sat empty. The practitioner was paid regardless.

A new receptionist is on their third week. A patient calls asking about a specific treatment. The receptionist doesn't know the answer, puts them on hold, walks through the clinic looking for someone who does, comes back two minutes later and gives a half-answer. The patient books somewhere else.

### What Keeps Them Up at Night

Not the clinical side. The invisible losses. The phone calls nobody answered. The no-shows they could have prevented. The patient who had a bad experience at the front desk and left a Google review about it. The realisation that they are entirely dependent on whoever showed up that morning — and that person is human, inconsistent, and expensive to replace.

The deeper fear: they've tried hiring better receptionists. It takes three months to onboard someone properly. By month four, the good ones leave for somewhere that pays more. They are stuck in a loop they can see clearly but can't get out of.

### Why They Haven't Solved It Yet

They've looked at software. The booking platforms — Fresha, Timely, TreatWell — handle scheduling but not the human layer. They still need someone to answer the phone, handle the exceptions, manage the questions that fall outside the system. The software replaced the calendar. It didn't replace the receptionist.

They've thought about outsourced reception services. But the idea of a call centre agent who doesn't know their clinic, their practitioners, their specific treatments, answering their patients' calls feels worse than the current problem.

They haven't seen a solution that felt built for them specifically. Everything they've encountered is either too generic, too expensive for what it delivers, or too complicated to trust.

### The Real Objection

It's not budget. It's reliability. The question underneath every "we'll think about it" is: *what happens when it breaks?*

They are already living with a system that breaks — human receptionists who forget, miss calls, make errors. But they understand human failure. They know how to manage it. What they don't know is what AI failure looks like, who is responsible when it happens, and whether they'll even be told it's happening.

**The answer:** The system runs at 99.5% uptime. When it is unavailable, calls are automatically diverted to the main line — no call is ever truly lost. The client is notified proactively if anything changes. This is more reliable than a receptionist who goes on lunch break, calls in sick, or is already on another call.

The objection is not a reason to say no. It is a reason to ask the question — and we have the answer.

### The Transformation They Want

**Before:** Two receptionists, two shifts, no consistency. Missed calls, wrong bookings, no-shows that could have been prevented, patient experience that varies depending on who picked up the phone. The owner carrying the mental load of a problem they cannot fix by working harder.

**After:** Every call answered. Every appointment reminder sent. Every booking logged correctly with the right practitioner and the right notes. The owner walks into the clinic on Monday morning and the weekend's enquiries are already handled. They stop losing revenue they never knew they were losing.

They don't want AI. They want a receptionist who never has a bad day, never forgets, never leaves, and costs less than the one they've already got.

## SECONDARY ICA: FOOD WHOLESALER (B2B)

A small to medium food wholesale business selling to other businesses — restaurants, cafes, retailers. Their team is lean. Inbound order management, account queries, and follow-up are handled manually, creating errors and delays that cost them client relationships built over years. The AI layer here is less about voice and more about automated order tracking, follow-up, and query handling. A different product conversation from clinics — pursue separately once primary ICA is closed.

## TERTIARY ICA: RESTAURANTS AND CAFES

Same missed call problem as clinics — reservations lost, enquiries unanswered, follow-up never sent. Average call value is lower than clinics, margins are thinner, and the owner is harder to reach (on the floor during peak hours). Worth pursuing opportunistically. Not the primary outreach target until a clinic case study is closed.

## WHO TO AVOID

- Barbers and solo tradespeople (electricians, plumbers) — low volume, low call value, resistant to tech investment
- NHS-linked GP practices — regulatory and procurement barriers
- Businesses where the decision maker is not the owner — too many stakeholders, too slow
- Anyone who leads with "how much does it cost" before understanding what it does
