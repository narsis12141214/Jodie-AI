## HOOK WRITING RULES

This is the most important skill in this agent's toolkit. A bad hook tells people what the content is about. A good hook makes them need to know what happens next. Every hook must pass the gap test before being used.

### The Gap Test

A hook works when it creates a gap between what the reader knows and what they want to know. If the reader can move on without feeling like they missed something, the hook failed.

**Bad hook (tells them what it's about):**
- "Today I'm going to talk about no-shows in dental practices."
- "Here are 5 ways to reduce missed calls."
- "A dental practice with 10 no-shows a week is losing £3,000 a month."

**Good hook (makes them need to know):**
- "Nobody tracks this number, but it is costing dental practices more than rent."
- "I showed a clinic owner one number from his own business. He went quiet for about ten seconds."
- "Nobody told me this about personal branding, and it cost me two years."

### Hook Mechanics

A strong hook uses one or more of these mechanics:

| Mechanic | What it does | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Curiosity gap** | Hints at something without revealing it | "Nobody tracks this number, but it is costing dental practices more than rent." |
| **Personal story entry** | Drops you into a moment | "I showed a clinic owner one number from his own business. He went quiet for about ten seconds." |
| **Unexpected contrast** | Sets up a belief, then breaks it | "A restaurant owner asked me to fix their marketing. I looked at their data and told them marketing was not their problem." |
| **Specific result** | A concrete number that demands explanation | "I gained 120,000 followers in under a year." |
| **Confession/vulnerability** | Admits something surprising | "Nobody told me this about personal branding, and it cost me two years." |
| **Challenge to assumption** | Directly contradicts what the reader believes | "The most expensive employee in your clinic is not the one with the highest salary." |

### Hook Rules

1. Maximum 2 sentences. One is better.
2. Never start with "Today I'm going to..." or "Here are X tips for..."
3. Never reveal the answer in the hook. The hook is the question, not the answer.
4. The hook must work on its own without the rest of the post. If someone reads only the hook and scrolls away, they should feel like they missed something.
5. Every hook must be pressure-tested before use. Ask: "Would I stop scrolling for this?" If the answer is no, rewrite it.
6. Use numbers when they are specific and surprising. Round numbers ("5 tips", "10 ways") feel generic. Specific numbers ("£3,247", "120,000", "ten seconds") feel real.
7. Name the person or industry in the hook when possible. "A dentist told me..." is more specific than "A business owner told me..."

### Hook Creation Process

1. Write the post body first. Identify the single most surprising or compelling detail.
2. Build the hook around that detail, but do not give it away.
3. Test the hook against all 6 mechanics above. Which one is it using?
4. If it does not clearly use at least one mechanic, rewrite.
5. Read the hook out loud. If it sounds like a headline from a blog post, it is too safe. Rewrite.

## LinkedIn Post Format

LinkedIn posts follow different rules from Instagram. The audience is business owners and decision makers. The tone is founder-led, honest, and direct.

### LinkedIn Format Rules

- Line break between every 1-2 sentences. Dense paragraphs die on LinkedIn.
- Short first line. This is the hook. It determines whether they click "see more."
- No links in the body. LinkedIn throttles reach on posts with outbound links. Put links in the first comment if needed.
- No hashtags in the first month of posting. After that, 3 maximum.
- No em dashes. Ever. Use commas, periods, or colons.
- Under 200 words per post.
- End with a soft CTA or open question, not a hard sell.

### LinkedIn Post Brief Format

```
LINKEDIN POST BRIEF - [Topic]
PILLAR: [Problem / Proof / Education / Positioning / Curiosity]
PUBLISH: [Day and date]
HOOK MECHANIC: [Curiosity gap / Personal story / Unexpected contrast / Specific result / Confession / Challenge]

HOOK:
[1-2 sentences maximum]

BODY:
[Full post text, line breaks included]

CTA:
[Soft close or open question]

FIRST COMMENT:
[Link or additional context if needed]
```

### LinkedIn Weekly Schedule

- **Monday:** Industry insight with a specific number. Problem or Education pillar.
- **Thursday:** Behind-the-scenes win, build, or founder story. Proof or Positioning pillar.
